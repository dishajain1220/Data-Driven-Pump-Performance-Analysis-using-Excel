# 📊 Data-Driven Pump Performance Analysis using Excel

A data-driven engineering project focused on evaluating the real operating performance of a centrifugal pump using **Microsoft Excel**. The project analyzes experimental pump test data to assess hydraulic performance, calculate efficiency, identify the **Best Efficiency Point (BEP)**, and provide insights for energy optimization and predictive maintenance in industrial pumping systems.

---

## 📌 Project Overview

Centrifugal pumps are among the most widely used rotating equipment in industries such as **Oil & Gas, Chemical Processing, Power Plants, Water Treatment, and Manufacturing**. Their performance directly impacts energy consumption, equipment reliability, and operational efficiency.

This project goes beyond theoretical pump analysis by using experimental data collected from a centrifugal pump test rig. Engineering calculations, statistical analysis, and Excel visualizations were used to evaluate pump performance under different operating conditions and determine the most efficient operating region.

---

## 🎯 Project Objectives

- Analyze the real operating performance of a centrifugal pump.

- Generate engineering insights to support energy optimization and predictive maintenance.

## ⚙ Engineering Calculations

The following performance parameters were calculated using standard fluid mechanics equations:

### ✔ Flow Rate (Q)

Calculated from:

> Water Collected / Time

---

### ✔ Total Dynamic Head (TDH)

Calculated using suction and delivery pressure readings to determine the energy imparted to the fluid.

---

### ✔ Hydraulic Power

Calculated using:

> **Hydraulic Power = ρgQH**

where:

- ρ = Density
- g = Gravity
- Q = Flow Rate
- H = Total Dynamic Head

---

### ✔ Shaft Power

Determined using electrical input measurements to represent the mechanical power supplied to the pump.

---

### ✔ Pump Efficiency

Calculated using:

> **Efficiency = Hydraulic Power / Shaft Power × 100**

The experimental analysis achieved an efficiency of approximately **56.92%**, indicating that nearly 57% of the supplied mechanical energy was converted into useful hydraulic energy. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

## 🔍 Key Insights

- Increasing valve opening increased the discharge (flow rate).
- Higher flow rates resulted in a gradual decrease in total dynamic head.
- Hydraulic power increased with discharge.
- Pump efficiency increased initially, reached its maximum at the **Best Efficiency Point (BEP)**, and then declined.
- Mechanical power supplied was significantly higher than hydraulic power due to unavoidable losses such as friction, leakage, and turbulence.
- Performance curves clearly demonstrated the relationship between flow rate, head, power consumption, and efficiency, enabling data-driven engineering decisions. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
